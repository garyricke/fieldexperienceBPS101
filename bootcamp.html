Week 1 of 3

Student Ownership & Mindset

This week is about you. Before you walk into any workplace, you need to understand who you are as a worker, what you want to get out of this experience, and how to take ownership of your learning. Nobody is going to hand you a great semester — you build it.

Lessons

1

What is Field Experience — Really?

The difference between showing up and actually growing

+

Field Experience is not a job shadow. It's not just "going somewhere for class." It's a real placement — at a real organization — where real professionals will expect real things from you. The way you perform here can shape references, recommendations, and your own understanding of what you want to do after high school.

Illinois BPS101 built this program specifically to meet the College and Career Pathway Endorsement (CCPE) requirements, which means completing your placement earns you a formal credential on your diploma. Employers and colleges recognize it. That's not nothing.

But here's the truth: you will get exactly as much out of this as you put in. Students who treat it like a class they have to survive get very little. Students who treat it like an opportunity they chose — who show up curious, ask questions, and push themselves — leave with something real.

The Core Idea

This semester isn't happening to you. You're the one driving. Your supervisor, your school, and this curriculum are resources — but the ownership is yours.

2

Know Yourself First

Self-assessment before your placement

+

The most self-aware workers outperform workers with more experience. Self-awareness means you know your strengths and you know your gaps — and you're honest about both.

Before you walk into your placement, think about these honestly:

Your Strengths

  • • What do you do well naturally?
  • • Where have teachers said you shine?
  • • What do you bring to a team?

Growth Areas

  • • Where do you tend to check out?
  • • What feedback do you usually get?
  • • What feels hardest about "being professional"?

Neither list makes you more or less ready for this. Knowing both makes you far more ready than someone who hasn't thought about it at all.

3

Setting Goals That Actually Work

SMART goals for your semester

+

Most people set vague goals. "I want to do well." "I want to learn stuff." These goals can't be measured, which means you'll never know if you hit them.

SMART goals are: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound. They sound corporate, but they work — and your supervisor will be thinking in terms like these when they evaluate you.

For your placement, try setting three types of goals:

L

Learning Goal

Something you want to understand or learn to do. "By the end of Week 4, I will understand how [specific task] works."

R

Relationship Goal

Someone you want to connect with. "I will introduce myself to three coworkers and have a real conversation with each one."

S

Skill Goal

Something you want to get better at. "I will practice asking follow-up questions instead of just saying 'okay' when I get instructions."

Week 1 Activity

Write Your Work Bio

A Work Bio is a 2–3 sentence professional introduction you could say out loud on your first day. It answers: Who are you? What are you here to do? What are you hoping to learn? Write it like you'd say it to your supervisor — professional, confident, and real.

Example (not yours — write your own):

"Hi, I'm Jordan. I'm a junior at Batavia High School and I'm in the Field Experience program this semester. I'm really interested in healthcare and I'm hoping to get a better sense of how hospitals actually run day-to-day. I'm a hard worker and I learn fast — I'm excited to contribute wherever I can."

This will be included in your Week 1 completion submission.

Week 2 of 3

Professional Skills & Workplace Readiness

This week is about the unwritten rules. Every workplace has a culture — norms around how you communicate, how you show up, and how you carry yourself. The students who learn these quickly are the ones who get opportunities. This week gives you the foundation.

Lessons

4

Professionalism — What It Actually Looks Like

Beyond "dress nicely and be on time"

+

When people say "be professional," they usually mean something much bigger than clothes and punctuality. Professionalism is how you carry yourself across every situation — especially the hard ones.

Illinois's Essential Employability Skills Framework identifies four pillars of professionalism that every employer uses to evaluate interns and new employees:

Personal Ethic

Integrity, respect for people and property, positive attitude, accepting accountability for your decisions.

Work Ethic

Dependability, predictable behavior, meeting deadlines, showing up prepared. Communicating proactively when you can't.

Communication

Active listening, clear verbal and written communication, professional email and digital etiquette.

Teamwork

Cooperating toward shared goals, asking questions to clarify, handling conflict respectfully and constructively.

These four pillars aren't just concepts — they're what your supervisor will use to evaluate you at the end of the semester.

5

Communication in the Workplace

Email, speaking up, and listening well

+

The #1 complaint employers have about new workers — including interns — is poor communication. Not showing up. Not asking for help when stuck. Sending unclear or informal messages. Not listening.

Communication at work looks different from communication with friends:

DON'T

"hey so i was gonna do the thing u told me but i forgot lol. when can we meet? also idk if i can come in thursday just a heads up"

DO

"Hi [Name], I wanted to follow up on the filing task we discussed on Monday. I have a question about Step 2 — would you have 5 minutes this week to clarify? Also, I may not be able to come in this Thursday due to a family commitment. I'll keep you updated. Thanks, [Your name]"

Key email rules: clear subject line, proper greeting, one topic per email, direct request, your name at the end. No slang, no lowercase "i," no "lol."

Also: asking for help is professional, not weak. The fastest way to waste time is to spend an hour doing a task wrong because you didn't want to "bother" your supervisor. Ask, confirm, then do it.

6

Workplace Rules You Need to Know

Attendance, phones, and what actually gets people in trouble

+

Every workplace has rules. Some are posted. Most aren't. Here are the ones that trip up interns most often:

Attendance & tardiness

Not showing up without warning is one of the fastest ways to damage your reputation. If you're going to be late or miss a day, contact your supervisor before it happens — not after. One text or email, as early as possible.

Phone use

Unless your supervisor has explicitly said phones are okay, treat your placement like a class — your phone stays away. If you're given a task, the expectation is that you're doing that task. Scrolling is visible and remembered.

Confidentiality

Some workplaces — especially in healthcare, law, and finance — have strict rules about what you can share. If you see patient info, client names, financial data, or internal documents: they stay at work. Don't post, screenshot, or tell friends.

Social media

Never post about your placement without permission — even positive stuff. Never tag the company, the staff, or share anything that happened during your day. Your supervisor may check.

Week 2 Activity

Write a Professional Email

You've been at your placement for two weeks. Your supervisor gave you a task last Friday, but you realized over the weekend that you misunderstood part of the instructions. It's Monday morning. Write a professional email to your supervisor explaining the situation and asking for clarification.

Remember to include:

  • • A clear subject line
  • • A professional greeting
  • • A brief explanation (no excuses, just facts)
  • • A specific question
  • • Your name

Lessons

7

Your Supervisor Relationship

What they need from you, and what you need from them

+

Your supervisor is not your teacher, your parent, or your friend. They're a professional who is investing time and trust in you. Here's what they actually need from you:

→

Reliability. Show up when you said you would. Do what you said you'd do. They need to trust your word.

→

Low maintenance. This doesn't mean never asking questions. It means not needing to be managed every second. Try things first, then ask.

→

Honest communication. If something is wrong — you're confused, overwhelmed, or made a mistake — tell them. Finding out later is always worse.

And here's what you need from them: clarity on expectations. If you don't know what "doing a good job" looks like in their eyes, ask them directly in your first week: "What would a great semester look like from your perspective?"

Getting feedback is part of the job. When they give you critical feedback, don't get defensive — get curious. "Can you show me what that would look like?" is one of the best responses to constructive criticism.

8

Navigating Day One

First impressions, logistics, and what to expect

+

First impressions form in seconds and take weeks to change. Your first day sets the tone for everything after it.

Before You Go

  • ✓ Know the address and how long it takes to get there
  • ✓ Arrive 10 minutes early
  • ✓ Dress appropriately for the industry
  • ✓ Bring a notebook and pen
  • ✓ Charge your phone the night before

When You Arrive

  • ✓ Introduce yourself confidently
  • ✓ Make eye contact and shake hands
  • ✓ Say your Work Bio (you practiced it in Week 1)
  • ✓ Take notes on names, locations, procedures
  • ✓ Observe before you opine

Questions to Ask

  • • "What does a great intern look like to you?"
  • • "What should I prioritize this first week?"
  • • "How do you prefer to communicate — email or text?"
  • • "Is there anything I should know about how things work here?"

You will feel awkward. That is normal. Push through it anyway.

9

Tracking Your Experience

Hours, reflections, and building your portfolio

+

The Illinois CCPE requirement is a minimum of 60 hours of supervised placement. Your school tracks this — but so should you.

Beyond hours, your placement is a story you're building. Each week, document:

→

What did you actually do? Specific tasks, not "helped out."

→

What did you learn? Something about the industry, the workplace, or yourself.

→

What challenged you? A moment where you had to figure something out or push past discomfort.

→

What's your goal for next week? One specific, actionable thing.

By the end of the semester, this is your portfolio — evidence of real growth in a real environment. It's the difference between saying "I did an internship" and being able to describe exactly what you built and who you became.

Your weekly check-ins with your teacher are also part of this.

Come with something real to say. The more honestly you reflect, the more support you'll get navigating challenges as they come up.

Week 3 Activity

Your First Week Plan

Before you go to your placement for the first time, create a brief written plan. This isn't a schedule — it's an intention. What do you want to accomplish in your first week? What impressions do you want to make? What questions will you ask?

Your plan should include:

  • • One thing you want your supervisor to know about you after Week 1
  • • Two questions you will ask in your first week
  • • One thing you're nervous about and how you'll handle it
